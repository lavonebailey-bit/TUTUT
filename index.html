Hey, did you know?

20 quick do’s and don’ts for a happier phone.

Small habits can make your phone faster, safer, cooler, and less frustrating.

🗺️ Do

Close navigation apps after your trip.

Maps and GPS keep using location in the background. Leaving them open can heat the phone and drain the battery faster.

📱 Don’t

Don’t leave social media videos running.

Video apps keep the screen, sound, internet, and processor busy. That can make the phone warm quickly.

🔄 Do

Restart your phone once a week.

A restart clears stuck background tasks and gives the phone a fresh start.

🧹 Don’t

Don’t install “cleaner” apps from ads.

Many cleaner apps show pop-ups, collect data, or make the phone feel worse. Use built-in Settings instead.

📦 Do

Keep at least a little storage free.

Phones slow down when storage is nearly full. Delete large videos or unused apps first.

🌡️ Don’t

Don’t ignore heat warnings.

If the phone says it needs to cool down, stop using it, remove the case, and keep it out of direct sun.

🏬 Do

Use official app stores.

Get apps from the Apple App Store or Google Play. Random download buttons are a common scam doorway.

🚨 Don’t

Don’t tap scary pop-up warnings.

Close the browser tab or app instead. Real companies do not ask you to call a number inside a pop-up.

⬆️ Do

Update your phone when it asks.

Updates fix security problems and often improve battery, speed, and reliability.

🔢 Don’t

Don’t share text-message codes.

A six-digit code is like a temporary key. Banks, Apple, Google, and Microsoft will not call to ask for it.

💡 Do

Turn down screen brightness indoors.

A bright screen uses a lot of battery and adds heat. Auto-brightness can help.

🔌 Don’t

Don’t charge under a pillow or blanket.

Charging creates heat. Keep the phone on a hard, open surface while it charges.

🎛️ Do

Check app permissions.

If an app does not need your location, camera, or microphone, turn that permission off in Settings.

🏦 Don’t

Don’t use public Wi-Fi for banking.

Public Wi-Fi can be less private. Use your home Wi-Fi or cellular connection for banking and medical accounts.

🔒 Do

Use a lock screen.

A passcode, fingerprint, or face unlock protects photos, messages, and accounts if the phone is lost.

🗝️ Don’t

Don’t use the same password everywhere.

If one account is stolen, different passwords help protect the others.

☁️ Do

Back up photos automatically.

iCloud, Google Photos, or another trusted backup can protect memories if the phone breaks.

⏸️ Don’t

Don’t answer pressure with speed.

Scammers rush people. Pause, hang up, and call the official number yourself.

🧵 Do

Clean the charging port gently.

Lint can stop charging. Never use liquid or metal. Ask a trusted repair shop if you are unsure.

🔕 Don’t

Don’t keep every notification on.

Too many alerts are stressful and use battery. Turn off notifications for apps you do not need immediately.

Social media tutorials

Understand what Facebook is showing you.

Facebook makes suggestions and chooses posts for your Feed. You are still in control of who you follow, what you see, and what you trust.

01

Facebook search is not Google.

Facebook search looks for people, posts, photos, Pages, groups, and other things inside Facebook. Its results are personalized by your Facebook activity. A result is not automatically true just because Facebook shows it.

  1. Check where you are. The Facebook search bar searches Facebook.
  2. Use your web browser for the wider internet. Google, Bing, or another search engine searches websites beyond Facebook.
  3. Verify important information. For health, money, government, or emergencies, visit the organization’s official website yourself.
02

Seeing someone does not mean you follow them.

“Suggested for You” and “People You May Know” are recommendations chosen by Facebook. Facebook can recommend people, Pages, groups, and posts you have never followed.

  1. Look for words such as “Suggested for You.” That means Facebook placed it there as a recommendation.
  2. You choose the connection. Only tap Follow, Add Friend, or Join when you recognize and trust the person or group.
  3. A suggestion is not a request you must accept. You can remove it, hide it, or keep scrolling.
03

Use the three dots to improve your Feed.

The three-dot button near the top-right corner of a post opens choices for that post. The exact words can differ by device or Facebook version.

  1. Tap the three dots. Look near the top-right of the post.
  2. Choose “Not interested,” “Show less,” or “Hide post.” This tells Facebook you want fewer posts like it.
  3. Use Report for harmful content. Report scams, impersonation, threats, or posts that may break Facebook’s rules.
04

AI videos can look and sound real.

Some AI-made or edited videos have visible mistakes, but the best fakes may not. Do not decide based on strange hands or lips alone. Verify the source before believing or sharing the video.

  1. Check the account. Is it the real person or organization? Look at its name, history, and official website.
  2. Look and listen carefully. Warning signs can include mismatched lips and audio, unnatural motion, warped text, changing backgrounds, or impossible details.
  3. Notice emotional pressure. Pause when a video demands urgent action, money, secrecy, or immediate sharing.
  4. Confirm with more than one reliable source. AI labels can help, but no label does not prove a video is real.

Best rule: Verify the source, not just the pixels.

Current guidance: Facebook Search, Facebook recommendations, Not interested, CISA manipulated media guidance.

AI and social scam safety

A familiar face or voice can still be fake.

Scammers can copy voices, use convincing video, take over real social accounts, and create fake profiles. You do not have to decide during the call or message.

STOP
Stop, hang up, and verify another way.

Call the person, bank, or company using a number you already know or find on its official website. Never use the number or link supplied by the unexpected caller.

☎

AI voice calls and family emergencies

A scammer may clone a relative’s voice from a short online recording. The voice may cry, whisper, or claim there was an accident, arrest, kidnapping, or medical emergency.

  • Urgency, secrecy, and “you are the only person who can help” are major warning signs.
  • Hang up and call your relative using the number already saved in your contacts.
  • Ask a private family question or use a family safe word. Do not send gift cards, cryptocurrency, cash, or a security code.
▣

Fake FaceTime and video calls

A video call can use a stolen account, prerecorded video, face filters, or AI. A bank, Apple, the police, or the government should not unexpectedly FaceTime you for money or account access.

  • Do not share your screen, bank app, password, passcode, or text-message security code.
  • Do not allow remote screen control for someone who contacted you unexpectedly.
  • End the call and contact the organization through its official app, card, statement, or website.
IG

Instagram scams

Watch for fake account-security warnings, paid verification offers, celebrity impersonators, romance messages, cash-flipping investments, and shops that move payment outside Instagram.

  • Instagram says it will not send account-security warnings through a direct message.
  • Never send a password, login link, security code, deposit, or “release fee.”
  • Open the profile’s three-dot menu to inspect or report the account. Check official Instagram emails inside Settings.
SC

Snapchat scams

Common traps include fake Snapchat support, phishing links, a hacked friend asking for money, fake giveaways, and threats involving private pictures.

  • Snapchat staff will not contact you through a Snap or Chat to provide support.
  • Back out if Snapchat warns that a link may be dangerous. Do not enter your login information.
  • Press and hold suspicious content or messages, then choose Report. Do not pay someone who threatens or blackmails you.
TT

TikTok scams

Beware of messages offering free followers, likes, Coins, prizes, brand jobs, investment returns, or account verification through another website.

  • TikTok says legitimate messages will not ask for your password or sensitive personal information.
  • Do not open suspicious messages, videos, or links asking you to enter TikTok login details.
  • Delete or report message requests from strangers who quickly ask for money, private contact, or personal details.
!

Facebook, Messenger, WhatsApp, dating apps, and every social platform

The same scams travel between apps: fake Marketplace payments, “new phone number” family messages, romance scams, investment groups, fake support, prizes, and requests to move the conversation elsewhere.

  • A payment screenshot is not proof. Check your bank or payment app yourself.
  • Someone who builds trust and then asks for travel money, medical money, investments, gift cards, or cryptocurrency may be running a romance scam.
  • Block and report the account. Tell someone you trust before sending money or private information.
♡

If an ad says “Hot singles in your area are waiting,” the only thing definitely waiting is the payment page. Cupid does not need your credit card number.

Safety sources: FTC family emergency scams, Apple FaceTime and support scams, Instagram scam guidance, Snapchat reporting, TikTok phishing guidance.
